Precautions to be followed by pregnant women.

Pregnant women are exceptional beings who carry life and are responsible for the growth and development of a newborn. During pregnancy, it is important for women to take care of their health and well-being to ensure a healthy and successful pregnancy.
Pregnancy is a time of transformation for a woman's body, and it's important to understand the changes that occur during this time. Hormones are constantly changing, and this can cause physical and emotional changes. Pregnant women may experience nausea, fatigue, pain and mood swings.
It is also important for pregnant women to take care of their diet by consuming foods rich in essential nutrients such as vegetables, fruits, proteins and complex carbohydrates. Pregnant women should also drink enough water to maintain adequate hydration.
Rest is also important for pregnant women. Pregnant women should get enough sleep to rest and help maintain their energy and mood. Regular exercise is also recommended for pregnant women to maintain their physical and emotional health.
It is important that pregnant women receive regular prenatal care from trained health professionals. Prenatal visits allow healthcare professionals to monitor the health of the mother and fetus and identify any potential problems.
Finally, it is important for pregnant women to focus on their emotional well-being. Pregnancy can be a stressful time, so it's important for pregnant women to take time to relax and focus on their mental health. Pregnant women can talk to their partner, family, or healthcare professional for support.
In summary, pregnant women need to take care of their physical and emotional health to ensure a healthy and successful pregnancy. Regular prenatal care, a healthy diet, adequate sleep and regular exercise are key elements for a successful pregnancy.

Symptoms of anemia after childbirth, the most famous methods of treatment.

Postnatal Anemia The fetus obtains all of its nutrient and vitamin needs from the mother's body, making the pregnant woman deficient in important minerals and vitamins throughout pregnancy and the postpartum months in particular. Iron deficiency anemia During pregnancy or after childbirth, the mother develops iron deficiency anemia when the body lacks the iron needed to produce hemoglobin, the body needs hemoglobin to make red blood cells which provide iron. oxygen to body cells. Pregnancy essentially contributes to low levels of iron in the mother, This deficiency increases with bleeding occurring during childbirth, or with the birth of twins, this post natal anemia due to iron deficiency can last from 6 to 12 months .

Babies are emotional sponges.

• Baby in society :
Baby grows and must learn to live in society. Little by little, he will learn to behave appropriately with those around him. All babies are formed for life in society through a phase of opposition where they say "no" to everything, through rivalries between siblings, and by testing your limits (he lies, does stupid things, tries blackmail ). Be firm, but watch out for hurtful words you might say. And to fully understand your child, know how to analyze the father/daughter or father/son relationships, as well as his status as an only child, if applicable.

• Educate Baby :
You have read and heard absolutely everything about parenting before becoming parents yourself. But once Baby arrived, you quickly understood that there was a world between theory and practice. At first, the basic rule to avoid conflicts with your Bout'chou is to avoid them with your spouse. It is likely that you did not receive the same education during your youth. Maybe he had strict parents and you had a rather "cool" and lax mom and dad. Nothing abnormal therefore to have different approaches to the education to be given to Baby. However, keep in mind that in front of your child, you must be one. Only one voice must be heard or you will lose all credibility with your child. Consult our article “education: when we disagree” to find out all about the subject. Dangerous games, education through “no”, swear words, hurtful comparisons… Discover all our other content related to the good education of children.

• Jealousy in Baby :
Jealousy affects all age groups, but it is essentially that of children that we want to talk to you about. Generally, jealousy begins to set in in a family when a second child is on the way. And all the paradox is there. The eldest ardently wishes to have a little brother or a little sister and once granted, he finally wants to return it to the store or get rid of it altogether. A perfectly normal reaction since the eldest has by definition been the center of attention for months and months. The questions then swirl around in his head: do I still have the same place in the family? Do you still love me so much? Why do they take care of me a little less? In these cases, the best solution is still dialogue. There is no need to make your child feel guilty or risk exacerbating the tensions between the eldest and the youngest. Consult our special files to learn all about jealousy in children and also benefit from the answers of our experts to questions from Internet users.

• The opposition phase :
Ah, the famous “opposing phase”, every parent’s nightmare. For those to whom the name does not mean anything, know that it designates the moment when your baby has only one word in his mouth: “no! A very large majority of toddlers go through this phase. It occurs around 18 months. According to specialists, it is nevertheless essential, for three reasons. On the one hand because it allows Baby to become aware of his own identity, but also to gain autonomy vis-à-vis his parents. Finally, the opposition phase allows him to test his parents and discover their limits... "The intensity of the opposition depends on the character of the child, but also, and perhaps above all, on the way in which the parents manage the crisis. Reveals child psychiatrist Bérengère Beauquier-Macotta. To discover our entire interview, consult our special file “Baby keeps saying no”. Then continue your navigation by consulting our other articles to know everything about the opposition phase of children.

• Baby's fears :
If your child checks under his bed and in his closet daily before going to bed, he is certainly not quiet at night. Fear of the dark ? Afraid of a monster hiding somewhere? Don't worry, it's very common, most children try to delay bedtime every night to avoid being alone in the dark. But where does this frequent fear of darkness come from? Patricia Chalon, psychotherapist and early childhood specialist, offers you explanations and advice in our article entitled “Fear of the dark: how to reassure your child? ". Obviously, this fear of darkness can sometimes disturb Bout'chou's sleep, and therefore lead to nightmares. In our special file dedicated to bad dreams, you will discover their origin, and especially how to prevent them from coming back, using simple and effective methods. But children's fears don't always stem from sleep and darkness, water can also be a source of worry.

• Baby's character :
Jealous, shy, bad player, depressed, funny... Your child necessarily has a dominant character trait. Take shyness, for example. Like all the other character traits mentioned above, it obviously has nothing hereditary. It is possible to overcome it if you do it well, without dramatizing the situation. Well yes, being shy is not a “fault”. Shyness can be linked to sensitivity or even respect. What attitude to adopt in public? Should you overprotect your child? How to avoid isolation? Find all our answers in our article entitled “Helping your child overcome his shyness”. Another well-known character trait in children (and not only for that matter): jealousy. “Being jealous is quite natural and even inevitable, reveals psychologist Lyliane Nemet-Pier. Babies are emotional sponges.

Vaccinations are essential for babies and children. They provide effective and lasting protection against various serious infectious diseases, sometimes even fatal for the little ones.

• GENERAL RECOMMENDATIONS
Vaccinations against diphtheria, tetanus, poliomyelitis, pertussis, Haemophilus influenzae b infection, hepatitis B, meningococcus C, pneumococcus, measles, mumps and rubella are compulsory for infants born in from January 1, 2018.
For children born before January 1, 2018, only vaccinations against diphtheria, tetanus and poliomyelitis were compulsory. Others were recommended.
Vaccination against human papillomavirus (HPV) infections, recommended for girls since 2007, has also been recommended for boys since January 2021. It is recommended for girls and boys aged 11 to 14.
Vaccination against meningococcal B infections has been recommended since April 2022, with two doses followed by a booster dose.
Vaccination against rotavirus gastroenteritis has been recommended since June 2022. The vaccine is administered orally, in two or three doses depending on the vaccine.

Ovarian syndrome.

Polycystic Syndrome (PCOS) is the most common hormonal disease in women of childbearing age. It can lead to fertility and hair disorders (hirsutism), as well as metabolic complications (diabetes). To date, there is no specific treatment. But ongoing research could be a game-changer by improving the still imperfect understanding of the mechanisms behind this disease.

SYMPTOMS OF POLYCYSTIC OVARIAN SYNDROME:
Symptoms of PCOS usually appear in adolescence, around the time of the first period, but can also occur much later. They vary greatly from person to person and are not all felt with the same intensity. Indeed, SPOK can, in some women, be particularly disabling.
In addition, they are not necessarily all present, but each of them must however evoke the diagnosis of PCOS.

• Disruption of the menstrual cycle :
Irregular menstrual cycles, which last more than 35 to 40 days and this, since adolescence; even by the total absence of periods (amenorrhea).

• Periods :
It is composed of 2 phases separated by ovulation:
Follicular phase, ovulation, Luteal phase
In PCOS, the basal level of LH is abnormally high in the majority of cases. But it does not increase in the middle of the cycle when it is this phenomenon that triggers ovulation.

• Hyperandrogenism :
In the presence of PCOS, the ovaries secrete too many androgens and particularly testosterone. From puberty, this disturbance can be responsible for:
hyperpilosity, even hirsutism in 70% of women then presenting with excess hair on the face, chest, back, buttocks; oily skin and the appearance of acne in adolescence and which persists after the age of 20; hair loss (alopecia) on the top of the skull and at the level of the frontal gulfs.

• Other Associated Symptoms in PCOS :
Other associated symptoms may occur but remain inconsistent, such as: dark spots that appear on the skin, most often on the back of the neck, under the arms and in the groin area (inner thighs). They are called "acanthosis nigricans" and are the consequence of a high level of insulin; depressed mood; anxiety; sleep apnea especially in case of overweight.

The treatment of PCOS is only symptomatic and this until menopause. It is based on an improvement in lifestyle, drug treatment in the event of hirsutism and/or infertility, and psychological support when necessary.
In case of overweight, a loss of about 10% of the initial weight reduces hyperandrogenism and shows a beneficial effect on amenorrhea, with a potential benefit on fertility. In the longer term, this weight loss will have a positive impact on the risk of metabolic complications associated with PCOS. On the other hand, for women whose weight is normal, losing weight does not bring any benefit.
In case of hirsutism, an estrogen-progestogen pill is recommended as first-line treatment. Its progestogen component inhibits the secretion of LH and reduces the production of ovarian androgens. The estrogenic component decreases the level of circulating androgens. If the estrogen-progestogen pill fails, treatment is based on an anti-androgen (cyproterone acetate) combined with a natural estrogen. Cyproterone acetate is effective in three months on acne and in six months on hirsutism.
As for metabolic abnormalities, they are treated by first-line dietary and lifestyle measures, then by oral antidiabetic drugs if necessary.

Breast cancer.

Breast cancer results from a disruption of certain cells which multiply and most often form a mass called a tumour. There are different types that do not evolve in the same way. Some are “aggressive” and evolve very quickly, others more slowly. Cancer cells can remain in the breast. They can also spread to other organs which is an even more threatening situation. We then speak of metastases. In the majority of cases, the development of breast cancer takes several months or even years.
Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women. It accounts for more than a third of all new cases of cancer in women. Symptoms of breast cancer are:
a hard mass in a breast, fixed or mobile, often with irregular contours, swollen and hard lymph nodes, not painful, in the armpit, spontaneous discharge from one of the two nipples, a change in a nipple or the appearance of the breast skin. When breast cancer has spread to the rest of the body, other symptoms may appear: fatigue, nausea, weight loss, bone pain or vision problems.
